I am a PhD candidate in Chemical & Petroleum Engineering at the University of Pittsburgh, working in the McKone Lab under the advisement of Dr. James McKone and Dr. Götz Veser. My doctoral thesis focuses on engineering nickel-based cathodes for alkaline hydrogen chemistry — a critical area within the broader landscape of clean energy technology.

My research spans the complete arc from fundamental catalyst design to practical electrolyzer performance: synthesizing Ni–Mo composite electrocatalysts, elucidating degradation mechanisms, and developing novel electroanalytical methodologies for benchmarking catalyst performance under realistic operating conditions.

Prior to Pitt, I completed my Bachelor of Chemical Engineering at the Institute of Chemical Technology (ICT) Mumbai — one of India's premier chemical engineering institutions — where I conducted research on CO₂ utilization catalysis and graduated as a recipient of the Government of India undergraduate scholarship.

Beyond the laboratory, I am actively involved in the academic community as a teaching assistant for Chemical Engineering Thermodynamics and Systems Engineering & Controls, and as a mentor to four undergraduate researchers. I have presented my work at 12 conferences including AIChE and ECS annual meetings, and have been recognized with awards for Best Research Poster, Best Scientific Animation, and Best Scientific Journal Art at the University of Pittsburgh.

I am currently seeking postdoctoral positions and industry roles in the green hydrogen and electrochemical energy space, anticipated for late 2026. I welcome conversations about research collaborations, technical consulting, or full-time opportunities at the intersection of electrochemistry and clean energy.

Areas of Work

Research

Area 01

Alkaline Hydrogen Evolution

Engineering Ni–Mo composite and multimetallic nanoparticulate electrocatalysts with high activity and durability for the hydrogen evolution reaction in alkaline media.

Area 02

AEM Electrolyzer Performance

Benchmarking Ni- and Pt-based cathodes in practical alkaline anion exchange membrane water electrolyzers using a custom-designed modular MEA platform.

Area 03

Catalyst Synthesis & Scale-Up

Developing novel synthesis strategies that scale nanoparticulate catalyst production from milligrams to tens of grams for practical deployment.

Area 04

Degradation Mechanisms

Investigating operational and shelf-life degradation of Ni–Mo catalysts using chemical and magnetic signatures, combined with COMSOL simulations.

Area 05

Electroanalytical Methodology

Developing novel methods to filter and elucidate reaction kinetics in a three-electrode MEA configuration for accurate and reproducible benchmarking.

Area 06

CO₂ Utilization Catalysis

Protic ionic liquid and CeO₂ nanoparticle-based catalysts for CO₂ cyclocondensation and fixation, achieving greater than 99% substrate conversion.

Ni- and Pt-Based AEM Electrolyzer Cathodes

My Role

Lead Author
What This Paper Is About

A central challenge in deploying alkaline anion exchange membrane (AEM) water electrolyzers is selecting the right cathode catalyst. Platinum (Pt) is highly active but expensive; nickel-based catalysts (Ni, Ni–Mo) are low-cost but their practical performance in membrane electrode assemblies (MEAs) is less understood. This paper directly compares Ni- and Pt-based cathodes — not just in half-cell tests, but in realistic AEM electrolyzer conditions.

Key Finding

We found a significant disconnect between intrinsic half-cell activity and practical MEA performance. While Pt shows superior activity in idealized half-cell tests, Ni–Mo composites can match or approach Pt performance in practical AEM electrolyzer configurations — a critical insight for cost-effective green hydrogen production at scale.

My Contribution

As the lead author, I was responsible for the synthesis of Ni- and Pt-based catalyst materials, design and execution of all electrochemical experiments (CV, LSV, EIS) in both half-cell and MEA configurations, ex-situ characterization (SEM, XRD, ICP-OES), and the primary writing of the manuscript. I also developed the electroanalytical methodology used to decouple kinetic from transport contributions in the MEA data.

Why It Matters

This work provides a practical framework for catalyst selection in AEM electrolyzers, directly informing industry decisions on whether expensive Pt can be replaced by earth-abundant Ni-based materials — a key step toward affordable green hydrogen production.

What This Paper Is About

Platinum nanoparticles (Pt NPs) are benchmark catalysts for the hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) in electrochemical systems. However, practical electrolyzers often operate with impure electrolytes containing trace contaminants. This paper systematically investigates how unsupported Pt nanoparticles behave — and degrade — when exposed to unpurified alkaline electrolytes, bridging the gap between idealized lab conditions and realistic operating environments.

Key Finding

Unsupported Pt NPs undergo significant deactivation in unpurified electrolytes due to surface poisoning and particle dissolution — processes that are largely absent in high-purity conditions. The study identifies specific contaminant species responsible for activity loss and quantifies the extent of degradation, providing a clearer picture of how Pt performs under real-world electrolyzer conditions.

My Contribution

As a co-author, I contributed to the electrochemical characterization of Pt nanoparticles using cyclic voltammetry (CV) and linear sweep voltammetry (LSV), assisted with ICP-OES analysis to quantify Pt dissolution, and contributed to data interpretation and manuscript review. This work complemented my broader thesis work on benchmarking catalysts in realistic electrolyzer environments.

Why It Matters

Understanding Pt degradation under realistic conditions is essential for designing durable electrolyzers. This study underscores the importance of electrolyte purity in performance benchmarking and informs catalyst stability testing protocols for both academic and industrial applications.
