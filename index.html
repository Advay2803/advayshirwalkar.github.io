I build catalysts that make cheap nickel do what expensive platinum does — and I'm graduating December 2026, ready to bring that from the lab to the real world.

It started in school. I remember reading about galvanic cells and reaction spontaneity and thinking it looked like magic — chemical energy becoming electrical energy, seemingly out of nowhere. Then came the idea that flipped it: if you supply electricity, you can reverse the reaction entirely, make the non-spontaneous possible. That felt like something profound. Around the same time I watched Carl Sagan's Cosmos and discovered Michael Faraday — a bookbinder's apprentice who became one of the greatest experimentalists in history, driven entirely by curiosity. Something about that combination — the elegance of electrochemistry and the idea that curiosity is enough — stayed with me. You could say electrochemical forces drove me here.

I came to the University of Pittsburgh to pursue that intersection. My PhD thesis — Engineering Advances in Nickel-Based Cathodes for Alkaline Hydrogen Chemistry — sits squarely at the interface of chemical engineering and electrochemistry. Over four years I have taken Ni–Mo/C catalysts from milligram-scale synthesis to a 1000× scale-up in collaboration with Giner Inc., built and automated a custom electrolyzer testing platform, developed new analytical methods to diagnose catalyst failure, and shown that an earth-abundant nickel catalyst can match the performance of platinum inside a real hydrogen-producing device. Along the way I have had the privilege of mentoring six researchers, teaching two courses, and presenting this work at 16 conferences.

The hardest moment of the PhD was also one of the most formative. Early on I designed a GDE half-cell setup from scratch — and it failed. The plate clearance was wrong: the porous electrode was either under-compressed, killing electrical conductivity, or over-compressed, collapsing the very channels that let reactants reach the catalyst. Everything about the problem was new. There was no one to ask, no paper that covered it exactly. I was scared and excited in equal measure. I fixed it, rebuilt it, and in the process learned something I could not have learned any other way — that being the person who figures it out from first principles is exactly where I want to be.

Outside the lab I train, run, hike, and trek. I hold a karate black belt and a national gold medal — not because I am naturally gifted, but because I learned early that the only competition that matters is with who you were yesterday. That lesson lives in how I approach research: I do not measure progress against others, I measure it against what I understood last week. Physical challenge and mental challenge feel the same to me — both require you to keep moving when it gets hard, and neither lets you fake it.

I am looking for a role where I can keep building things that matter — in R&D or process development, in energy or advanced materials, starting January 2027. If that sounds like your team, I would genuinely love to talk.

Doctoral Research

Research

Thesis
Engineering Advances in Nickel-Based Cathodes for Alkaline Hydrogen Chemistry

Alkaline AEM electrolyzers offer a compelling path to low-cost green hydrogen, enabling earth-abundant transition metals — nickel and molybdenum — to replace platinum-group metals (PGMs): materials that are economically expensive and concentrated in geopolitically sensitive supply chains. However, non-PGM cathodes that simultaneously meet activity, durability, and manufacturability requirements for practical deployment remain elusive. My doctoral research addresses this gap across the full development chain: from catalyst synthesis and 1000× scale-up, to device-level benchmarking, to transport modeling, to understanding and mitigating degradation under both storage and operating conditions.

Catalyst Design & Scale-Up

Co-precipitation–thermal reduction of Ni–Mo/C composites — optimized for morphological control and scaled from mg to tens of grams in collaboration with Giner Inc.

Ni–Mo/CScale-UpGiner Inc.
Overview
Synthesis via co-precipitation–thermal reduction, optimized for morphological control. Scaled 1000× (mg → tens of grams) with Giner Inc., targeting a 20× reduction in cathode material cost vs. Pt-based PEM systems. Transitioning from batch furnace to plug-flow column to improve reproducibility — with fluidized bed as the next step toward kilogram-scale production.
Key result
Transfer-ready manufacturing protocols validated at both RDE and MEA device levels — delivered to Giner Inc. for commercial roadmap.
Thrust 02
▼

Device-Level Benchmarking

Head-to-head AEM electrolyzer comparison of Ni–Mo/C vs. Pt–Ru/C — Ni–Mo/C devices perform statistically indistinguishably from PGM benchmarks.

AEMMEAACS 2025
Overview
Custom modular three-electrode MEA system designed, built, and automated in-house — achieving 70× cost reduction vs. full-cell configurations. Novel electroanalytical methodology decouples kinetic from transport contributions in MEA data.
Key result
Three findings: (1) RDE activity is loading-dependent — Ni–Mo within 10-fold of Pt after correction; (2) PGM-optimized ionomer formulations uniquely suppress Ni–Mo activity; (3) AEM device performance of Ni–Mo/C is statistically indistinguishable from Pt–Ru/C.
Thrust 03
▼

Transport Modeling

Effectiveness factor analysis and COMSOL finite element modeling to decouple reaction kinetics from internal mass transport in porous catalyst films.

COMSOLRDEIn Prep
Overview
Effectiveness factor analysis from classical chemical reaction engineering applied to porous catalyst films. COMSOL FEM validated against experimental RDE data across loading ranges. Reveals hyperbolic dependence of apparent activity on loading — current configs operate below transport-limited regime.
Key result
Quantifies available headroom to reduce mass transfer artifacts. Framework generalizes beyond Ni–Mo/C to any porous electrocatalyst film — bridges RDE benchmarking and real device configurations.
Thrust 04
▼

Shelf-Life Stability & Reactivation

Oxidative degradation during storage tracked via magnetic signatures (SQUID, UMN). Reactivation protocol identified that exceeds original day-one activity.

Shelf-LifeMagneticJACS
Overview
Ni–Mo/C is surprisingly sensitive to ambient storage — oxidative degradation produces measurable changes in activity and bulk magnetic signatures within days. Tracked via electrochemistry, XRD/XPS/TGA, and SQUID magnetometry (Prof. Chris Leighton, UMN).
Key result
Reactivation protocol identified that restores — and exceeds — original day-one activity. Magnetic signatures provide a rapid, non-destructive quality control proxy for manufacturing contexts.
Thrust 05
▼

Operational Durability

Accelerated stress testing of Ni–Mo cathodes in alkaline HER via GDE half-cell platform — including impure and seawater feed streams.

ASTDurabilityGDE
Overview
GDE half-cell platform for accelerated stress testing (AST) tracks structural and chemical changes in Ni–Mo cathodes during alkaline HER. Extends to impure and seawater feeds — common in real-world deployment without expensive purification.
Key result
Ni–Mo exhibits higher resistance to chloride poisoning and dissolved iron contamination than Pt-based cathodes — a significant practical advantage for non-purified water sources.

What This Paper Is About

A central challenge in deploying AEM water electrolyzers is selecting the right cathode catalyst. This paper directly compares Ni- and Pt-based cathodes in both idealized half-cell tests and realistic MEA electrolyzer conditions.

Key Finding

A significant disconnect exists between half-cell activity and practical MEA performance. Ni–Mo composites can match or approach Pt performance in practical AEM electrolyzer configurations — critical for cost-effective green hydrogen production.

My Contribution

Lead author — responsible for catalyst synthesis, all electrochemical experiments (CV, LSV, EIS) in half-cell and MEA configurations, ex-situ characterization (SEM, XRD, ICP-OES), and primary manuscript writing. Developed the electroanalytical methodology to decouple kinetic from transport contributions in MEA data.

Why It Matters

Provides a practical framework for catalyst selection in AEM electrolyzers, informing industry decisions on replacing expensive Pt with earth-abundant Ni-based materials.

Systematically investigates how unsupported Pt nanoparticles behave and degrade in unpurified alkaline electrolytes, bridging the gap between idealized lab conditions and realistic electrolyzer environments.

Unsupported Pt NPs undergo significant deactivation in unpurified electrolytes due to surface poisoning and particle dissolution — processes largely absent in high-purity conditions.
